For each behaviour pattern generate:

2. A follow-up probe question to dig deeper if the answer is superficial. E.g.: "What was specifically your role in that?" / "What did you actually do when it went wrong?"
3. A 4-level scoring rubric defining what a 1, 2, 3, and 4 answer looks like for THIS specific pattern:
   - 1 (Insufficient): What a weak answer lacks
   - 2 (Below expectation): What an average but insufficient answer looks like
   - 3 (Meets expectation): What a satisfactory answer includes
   - 4 (Exceeds expectation): What an exceptional answer demonstrates

Rubric criteria must be role-specific and behaviour-oriented — not generic.

CRITICAL STAR PRINCIPLE — READ CAREFULLY:
The question must be SPECIFIC about the situation (a concrete context matching the pattern's domain) but OPEN about the action. The candidate decides what they did — that is the entire point of STAR.

FORBIDDEN in the main question: any phrasing that reveals the desired behaviour or expected answer, such as:
- "...and you took action to align with the team directly"
- "...without escalating to your manager"
- "...and you resolved it by..."
- "...you successfully avoided X"
- "...by communicating directly with..."
These phrases leak the desired answer and destroy 4-level scoring validity: the candidate simply repeats the question's wording and lands on Level 4 without having demonstrated the behaviour.

CORRECT FORMAT — example:
Wrong: "Tell me about a time when you identified that a dependency was at risk of slipping, and you took action to align with that team directly, without escalating to your manager first."
Right: "Tell me about a time when you spotted that a cross-team dependency was at risk of slipping. What did you do?"

The probe is where you dig deeper: "Walk me through exactly what you did — who did you contact first, and why? What happened as a result?"
