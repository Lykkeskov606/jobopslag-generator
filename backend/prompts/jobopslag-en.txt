━━━ VARIANT A — AIDA + WIIFM ━━━

Use the AIDA framework. Each layer has a specific purpose:

ATTENTION (opening, 1–2 sentences):
Speak DIRECTLY to the candidate's inner motivation or the core challenge this role involves — not what the company wants. NEVER start with the company name or "We are looking for". Strong examples: "There's a difference between shipping code and owning a system." / "Great HR people don't just fill roles — they change how teams are built." Formula: [candidate's role/identity] meets [the real challenge or opportunity].

INTEREST (role description, 2–3 paragraphs):
Describe what the candidate ACTUALLY does day-to-day — the most important responsibilities, who they work with, and what success looks like. Use active sentences and specific examples from the bullets. Avoid generalisations and corporate language.

DESIRE — WE OFFER: (dedicated section):
This is where WIIFM lives. List precisely and specifically what the candidate gains by saying yes:
• Concrete compensation range or "competitive salary + [specific benefits]"
• Real, specific growth and development opportunities — not generic ones
• Flexibility, culture, purpose, autonomy
• Practical benefits that differentiate this role from others

ACTION (call to action, 2–3 sentences):
One clear instruction. Tell the candidate what to send (CV, cover letter, portfolio). Warm, direct tone — not corporate boilerplate.

━━━ VARIANT B — TACTICAL EMPATHY + CIALDINI ━━━

Use a combination of tactical empathy (Chris Voss, "Never Split the Difference") and Cialdini's principles of persuasion. This variant must FEEL different from Variant A — more human, more curious about the candidate.

OPENING — Tactical empathy / labelling (1–2 sentences):
LABEL the candidate's likely professional feeling or frustration with their current situation. Show that you understand their inner world — you are NOT selling a job, you are acknowledging an experience.
Strong examples:
• "You probably know the feeling: you have the skills, but not the mandate to make a real difference."
• "It's hard to find a role that matches both the expertise you've built and the level of ownership you're ready for."
• "Most people with your background are sitting in roles that underestimate what they're actually capable of."
Use "you" throughout. Never open with the company's name or "We are a company that...".

THE ROLE — Commitment/consistency (Cialdini):
Describe the role by framing it around qualities the CANDIDATE likely already identifies with. Examples: "If you're the kind of person who thrives when..." / "You do your best work when...". This activates Cialdini's consistency principle: the candidate confirms that they fit. Describe the real responsibilities and day-to-day, always from the candidate's perspective.

SOCIAL PROOF (Cialdini):
Include one concrete, authentic signal about the culture from the INSIDE. What do people who work here say? What's the typical experience at 6 months in? Make it specific and credible — not "we have a great culture". Example: "Most people who join us say the same thing: they didn't know work could feel like this."

YOU'LL GET: (WIIFM section):
List at least 4 concrete benefits, compensation details, and opportunities. Apply Cialdini's liking principle in the wording: show that real people are offering this, not an HR department.

CLOSING — Invitation (not a demand):
Close with a WARM, PERSONAL invitation — not an "apply now" button. Reinforce Cialdini's liking by showing genuine curiosity about the candidate as a person, not just a set of skills.
Example: "Send us a message and tell us a bit about yourself — what you're doing now, and what you're really looking for."
